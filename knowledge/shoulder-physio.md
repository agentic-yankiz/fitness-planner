# Left Anterior (Front) Shoulder — Prehab / Rehab

> ⚠️ **Not a diagnosis.** Shaked reports a possible left **front** shoulder issue.
> This is conservative, evidence-aligned prehab built from reputable climbing-physio
> sources. **Get an in-person assessment** from a physiotherapist who works with
> climbers for a real diagnosis. Stop anything that causes sharp or lasting pain > ~3/10.

## Why front-shoulder pain happens in climbers
Common culprits behind anterior shoulder pain in climbers: subacromial/internal
**impingement**, **biceps (long head) tendinopathy**, labral irritation, and **rotator-cuff
or scapular control deficits**. A repeated theme in climbing rehab: anterior joint
stress drops when you strengthen the **mid-scapular muscles** (between the shoulder
blades) and the **rotator cuff**, restoring balance to a pull-dominant climber's shoulder.

## The principle that drives the block
**Strengthen the back of the shoulder and the scapular stabilizers** → the front of the
joint is pulled into a better position and offloaded. Conveniently, the **serratus /
scapular** work also builds the protraction the **planche** needs — one block, two wins.

## Rules while the shoulder is symptomatic
1. **Keep training pain-free movements.** Total rest is usually not the answer; load it
   in ranges that don't hurt.
2. **Ramp the aggravators slowly.** Planche leans, one-arm lock-offs, and deep/loaded
   anterior positions are the most likely to flare it — these progress *last*.
3. **Pain monitor:** during ≤ 3/10 and settled by next morning = OK to continue. Sharp,
   or worse the next day = regress.
4. **Warm up the shoulder before every session** (it's in the 8-min warm-up).
5. **See a physio** if pain persists > 2–3 weeks, wakes you at night, or includes
   weakness/instability — those warrant imaging/diagnosis, not more home prehab.
